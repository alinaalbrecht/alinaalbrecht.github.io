Savvie is a responsive web app that aims to ease the transition to adulthood by connecting young adults with education, career, and personal finance advisers. The app also provides text and video based resources, as well as a forum where young adults can exchange experiences and get advice from their peers. Savvie is aimed at students, young professionals, and expats between the ages of 18 and 30.

This is a project I designed in the context of the CareerFoundry UX Immersion course.

01 Discovery

The Problem…

challenges facing young adults, including moving away from home, entering higher education, starting a career, and managing finances can often feel overwhelming.

Unfortunately, it can be difficult to find a comprehensive online resource that addresses all of these areas of concern. While many young adults rely on family and friends for help, these trusted advisers may find it difficult to provide guidance if the young adult in their lives moves abroad or enters a career field that is foreign to them.

That’s where Savvie comes in. Savvie is a responsive web app that aims to provide support to young adults across the globe on topics ranging from moving away from home to personal finance. Users can contact advisers directly for one-on-one call sessions or browse text and video-based resources.

I began this project by conducting a competitive analysis of similar apps that match users with tutors or specialize in teaching users new skills through video instruction. This analysis allowed me to identify the strengths and weaknesses of my competition. It also illuminated what is missing in the online landscape of resources for young adults.

The competitive analysis helped clarify my ideas for my responsive web app. Based on my findings I drafted a problem statement that would guide my design concept.

Before diving into further research and interviewing potential users, I established the app’s key requirements as executable user stories.

User Stories

As a user, I want to be able to gain relevant insights into the topic at hand before contacting an expert, so I can receive useful information almost instantaneously without first scheduling a call.

As a user, I want the app to suggest experts to me whose expertise matches my profile, so that I don’t have to spend time scrolling through endless pages and comparing multiple experts with one another.

As a user, I want to be able to browse experts and get the most important information about them at a glance, so that I can quickly assess whether or not I want to know more about them.

User Interviews

Next, I conducted both an online survey as well as three in-depth interviews with young adults in their twenties.

I wanted to find out what struggles they associated with young adulthood and in which areas of their life they felt they needed support. Furthermore, I wanted to discover how to best address their needs in my app design.

Then I consolidated and analyzed my data using affinity mapping. This helped me uncover:

  • Which topics my app should cover.
  • Which modes of interaction with advisers would be most salient.
  • Which additional features would be most important to my prospective users.
  • The context in which prospective users would utilize the app.
  • Potential concerns and deal-breakers that would stop potential users from exploring the app.
  • Which payment plans prospective users preferred.

User Personas

Now I used my consolidated data to create three personas who each represented my distinct target audience groups: Students, recent university graduates, and young professionals.

Each persona has unique motivations, behaviors, and needs that I would need to account for in my app design.

Referencing these personas throughout the project have proved an effective way to empathize with prospective users and keep their needs in mind.

02 Concepting

User Journey Maps

In order to visualize the stages through which users progress as they pursue a particular goal I created user journey maps for each of my personas.

By considering how the persona would respond mentally and emotionally to each task they encountered along the way, I was able to identify which features were imperative to include for a seamless user experience.

After mapping the user journey for my persona Stuart, a recent university graduate who wants to find an adviser with whom he can discuss career planning, I identified the following opportunities:

  • Stuart needs to be able to see the adviser’s qualifications at a glance.
  • Stuart needs to be able to share pertinent information about himself with the adviser.
  • We can give Stuart personalized recommendations, so that he will find a qualified adviser faster.
  • Stuart needs effective filtering options to find the adviser best suited to his needs.

User Flows

Next I turned the user stories from my user journey maps into user flows in order to discover which screens would be necessary for a user to complete a given task. These user flows helped lay the foundation for planning the app’s information architecture.

Sitemap

After planning the initial information architecture I conducted an open card sort to help me refine the sitemap. Seven participants grouped 26 cards into categories that made sense to them.

The card sort helped me identify recurring grouping patterns. I used these results to make a few changes to my sitemap that would yield a more intuitive and logical user experience.

Low-Fidelity Wireframes

After reviewing usability heuristics, common design patterns, and deciding which navigation patterns would best suit the different features of my app, I began translating my user flows into paper wireframes for mobile and desktop.

These initial sketches allowed me to focus on the basic navigation, interaction, and placement of elements on the individual screens.

03 Prototyping & User Testing

High-Fidelity Wireframes & Prototype

Having already established the design patterns and rough placement of elements, I fine-tuned the sizing and spacing of elements in the high-fidelity wireframes and inserted real copy to give a better idea of what the look and feel of the final product would be.

Finally, I linked the wireframes into an interactive prototype using InVision.

Usability Testing

Now I was ready to conduct usability tests with six participants.

I asked my participants to complete four scenario-based tasks in order to assess their ability to execute basic tasks, such as creating an account, scheduling a call with an adviser, and reading an article.

I collected all my findings, both positive and negative, in a rainbow spreadsheet (left).

The tests helped me identify five main issues that I addressed in the next design iteration:

  1. Participants had trouble navigating the hamburger menu.
  2. Participants were uncertain about how to navigate subcategories in the career resources section.
  3. Participants wanted to enter more information about themselves for a more tailored experience.
  4. No average rating was displayed for the advisers.
  5. After scheduling a call some participants were unsure if the call was confirmed or not.

Revisions

After reviewing my findings from the usability tests, I made several revisions to the prototype.

On the right you can see two of the changes I implemented after receiving participants’ feedback. This is the screen the user sees after scheduling a call with the adviser Ben Adams.

Understandably some users were confused whether or not the call had been scheduled successfully, because there was no indication of it on the adviser’s profile. Therefore, to indicate that the user has scheduled a call, I changed the “schedule call” button to read “call booked” instead.

Another user pointed out that although she could read reviews about the adviser, she could not see their average rating. Therefore, I created a new icon to show the adviser’s average rating and positioned it near the top of the adviser’s profile.

04 User Interface Design

Visual Design

Now I was ready to bring color to the user interface. I decided to build my color palette around a warm yellow to create a welcoming, friendly atmosphere for users.

Because the app addresses topics that might make some users nervous, I implemented several strategies to put them at ease, including using a friendly, conversational tone throughout the app, using warm, non-obtrusive colors, and using illustrations instead of photos where possible.

For a clean, modern feel I chose to use the complementary sans-serif fonts Futura and Montserrat throughout the design.

In order to ensure a consistent layout I also made use of 12-column grids.

Final Touches

I revised the design further after collecting peer feedback and reviewing WCAG guidelines. The changes I made included fine-tuning the spacing of elements, improving system feedback, as well as improving the color-contrast ratio of text elements.

Design Language System

After polishing the UI design, I created a design language system document, in which I detailed how color, typography, UI elements, and language should be used throughout the app to achieve a cohesive product.
